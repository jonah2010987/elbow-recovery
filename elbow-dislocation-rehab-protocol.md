# Posterior Elbow Dislocation: Return-to-Climbing Protocol

**Injury:** Simple posterior dislocation (no fracture), ~5 June 2026. Cast removal ~19 June (end of week 2).
**Assumptions:** Simple dislocation confirmed on imaging; stability not formally tested. Confirm with York Fracture Clinic that there is no documented gross instability before starting Phase 2 loading. This is a framework to discuss with your physio, not a replacement for one.

**Anatomy note that drives everything below:** A posterior dislocation tears the LCL complex first (and often the MCL). The LCL is what fails in posterolateral rotatory instability. The positions that stress healing ligaments are: varus force (arm out to the side, gravity pulling the forearm down), valgus force, combined supination + extension + axial load, and passive end-range extension. The positions that are safe early are: overhead, pronated, actively moved. Stiffness, not re-dislocation, is the main enemy after a simple dislocation; an elbow immobilised past 3 weeks predictably loses extension.

## The hinged ROM brace (expected at cast removal): what it changes

The plan assumes you leave the clinic on ~19 June in a hinged brace with an extension block rather than free. This is standard practice, not necessarily a sign they think you're unstable, but ask directly: "is the brace precautionary or did you find instability?" and "what's my block schedule?" Their schedule overrides the generic one below.

What the brace actually does: the hinge allows flexion/extension in the sagittal plane while mechanically blocking varus/valgus, and the extension stop keeps you out of the end range where the healing posterior structures get stressed. A typical schedule blocks extension at around 30° and opens it roughly 10° per week so full extension is allowed by about 3 weeks in the brace.

Implications for the protocol:

- **It brings loading forward, modestly.** Most of the Phase 1 caution exists because an unprotected elbow sees varus stress in everyday positions. With the hinge handling that, dependent-position AROM is safe from day one in the brace (no need to wait until week 3–4), and the light Phase 2 Voltra menu (curls, pushdowns, rows, all mid-range, elbow tucked) can plausibly start ~1 week earlier, performed in the brace, if the pain rules are met and the clinic agrees. The rate limiter becomes tissue irritability rather than joint protection.
- **It does not bring everything forward.** The block means no end-range extension work until the block is opened, so the low-load long-duration extension stretching waits. It also doesn't license hangs, pressing, or anything supinated and loaded near extension; those stay on the Phase 3 timeline.
- **Respect the block, don't fight it.** Working hard against the stop is a varus/valgus-free way to irritate the joint and feed the HO risk. ROM gains come from frequency inside the allowed arc.
- **The overhead protocol still earns its place.** Even with a brace, the supine overhead position is the most protected place to chase flexion/extension reps; do it in the brace unless told otherwise.
- **Daily life normalises faster.** Carrying light loads and reaching out sideways are much safer braced, so the Phase 1 "avoid" list relaxes while it's on.
- **Keep it for the comeback.** If the clinic allows, the brace is a sensible thing to wear for the first few wall sessions in Phase 4; published protocols have athletes returning to sport in a hinged brace for months.
- **Weaning:** typically off for daily life around 4–6 weeks post-injury per clinic instruction. Expect the arm to feel oddly vulnerable for the first unbraced week; that's proprioceptive, not structural.

## Phase 1 — Weeks 2–4 post-injury (cast off): restore motion

**Goal:** Maximum active ROM without stressing the collaterals. Expect flexion to return quickly and the last 20–30° of extension to lag for weeks. That is normal. Do not chase it passively.

### Daily ROM work (4–6 short sessions/day, little and often beats one long session)

1. **Overhead flexion/extension (the Schreiber overhead protocol).** Lie supine, shoulder flexed to 90° (upper arm vertical, pointing at ceiling), forearm pronated. Actively bend and straighten the elbow. 10–15 slow reps per session. In this position gravity compresses the joint and stabilises it, so there are no ROM limits other than pain tolerance.
2. **Overhead pronation/supination.** Same position, elbow at 90°, rotate palm up/down. 10–15 reps.
3. **Dependent-position AROM.** Seated, upper arm supported on a table, actively extend as far as the brace block allows with the forearm pronated. Safe from day one if braced; from week 3–4 if not. Active only. No partner stretching, no pushing into end range with the other hand, no hanging a weight off the wrist.
4. **Wrist and hand.** Wrist flexion/extension AROM, putty or stress-ball grip work, finger extensions with a band. You already tolerate hard isometric grip, so grip can be loaded normally as long as the elbow stays at 90° at your side.

### Isometrics (daily, from cast removal)

- Biceps isometric at 90°: press wrist up into the other hand, 5 × 10 s at ~50–70% effort.
- Triceps isometric at 90°: press wrist down into the other hand or a table, same dosing.
- Shoulder isometrics in all directions with elbow supported.

### Swelling and comfort

- Elevate above heart when sitting/sleeping, ice 15–20 min as needed.
- Use the arm for light daily tasks below shoulder height with loads under ~1 kg.

### Avoid in Phase 1 (these apply with the brace off; the hinge covers the first two while it's on)

- Carrying anything with the arm hanging at your side in extension (varus + distraction).
- Reaching out sideways with the arm unsupported.
- Passive or partner stretching into extension, or fighting the brace's extension block.
- Any valgus/varus load, supinated loaded extension.

### Training you can keep doing

- Lower body: GHD work, belt-style or goblet-free patterns that don't load the arm. Back squat is out until the elbow tolerates the rack position (likely Phase 3); use the rack for split squats holding a dumbbell in the good hand, or load via a belt.
- Easy Z2 cardio (walking, incline treadmill). No running yet if it jars the arm; reassess week 4.
- Voltra single-arm work on the good side is fine and worth doing (cross-education effect is real, roughly 10–15% strength preservation in the immobilised limb).

### Red flags (contact the fracture clinic)

- Increasing pain *with* decreasing ROM week on week. This is the screen for heterotopic ossification (HO), where bone forms in the soft tissue around the joint and quietly steals extension. It is the single most important thing to watch for in the first 12 weeks. A posterior dislocation often bruises the brachialis (the muscle lying directly over the front of the joint), which is a recognised HO trigger. The defence is exactly the Phase 1 rule below: active motion, never forced passive stretching. Forceful, repeated cranking on a stiff elbow is shown experimentally to *drive* HO, so if extension is stubborn the answer is patience and low-load long-duration positioning, not aggression.
- Clunking, giving-way, or a sense the joint is shifting.
- Pins and needles or numbness in the ring/little finger (ulnar nerve).

## Phase 2 — Weeks 4–6: full active ROM, first external load

**Entry criteria:** AROM improving weekly, pain ≤3/10 during exercises, no instability symptoms. Ideally fracture clinic has confirmed a stable joint by now. If braced and the clinic agrees, the Voltra menu below can start ~1 week early, performed in the brace.

- Continue daily AROM until extension is within ~10° of the other side.
- **Low-load, long-duration extension work** if a deficit persists once the brace's extension block is fully open (or from week 5–6 if unbraced): arm supported on a bench, light cuff weight (0.5–1 kg) at the wrist, 3–5 min holds at comfortable end range, 2–3×/day. This is the evidence-based way to regain extension; ballistic stretching is not.
- **First Voltra loading, all in mid-range (roughly 30–120° flexion), elbow tucked, forearm neutral or pronated:**
  - Cable curl, neutral grip: 3 × 15–20 at the lowest meaningful resistance.
  - Triceps pushdown, elbow pinned to side: 3 × 15–20.
  - Seated row, elbows tracking close to ribs: 3 × 15.
  - Wrist curls and reverse wrist curls over a bench: 3 × 15–20.
  - Pronation/supination with the Voltra handle held hammer-style or a light hammer/heavy spanner: 3 × 10 each way, slow.
- Scapular and cuff work (band external rotation, serratus wall slides). The shoulder has been idle for a month and will matter on the wall.
- Pain rule for all loading: ≤3/10 during, settled by next morning. Next-day flare means repeat the same load, not progress.

## Phase 3 — Weeks 6–10: progressive strength and closed-chain stability

**Entry criteria:** Extension within ~10° of the contralateral side, full flexion and rotation, Phase 2 loads pain-free.

### Open-chain strength (3 sessions/week, Voltra-centric)

- Progress curls, pushdowns, rows from 3 × 15 toward 4 × 8–10, adding load when the top of the range is clean.
- From ~week 8, introduce Voltra eccentric overload mode on curls and pushdowns at modest settings; eccentrics are the stimulus the ligament and common flexor/extensor origins need, but they bite the next day, so start at 110–120% rather than max overload.
- Lat pulldown pattern (Voltra high anchor, kneeling) before any pull-up work.
- Pressing: incline push-ups against the rack rails, walking hands lower each week toward floor push-ups. Pressing is the closest gym analogue to the injury mechanism (extended arm, axial load), so it is deliberately the slowest progression. Floor push-ups pain-free is a key milestone.

### Closed-chain and proprioception (the bit generic NHS sheets skip and climbers need most)

- Quadruped weight shifts → quadruped with the good arm lifted → slow shoulder taps.
- Wall push-up holds with rhythmic perturbation (partner or self-generated mini-oscillations).
- Ball dribbles against a wall at shoulder height, 30–60 s sets.
- Bear crawl holds, short crawls from ~week 8.

### Hanging progression (gateway to climbing)

- Week 7–8: feet-on-floor "hangs" on a bar or jug, taking maybe 30–50% bodyweight, arms slightly bent.
- Week 8–9: progress depth of knee bend toward 70–80% bodyweight.
- Week 9–10: full bodyweight two-arm hang on jugs, slight elbow bend, build to 3 × 20 s pain-free. This is your ticket to the wall.
- No one-arm hangs, no campus, no max hangs in this phase. Crimpd's foundational grip/board-prep style sessions can resume here at low intensity if hangs are clean.

### Squat rack

- Reintroduce back squat once the rack position is comfortable (usually fine by week 7–8 since it is flexion + compression); safety bar or front squat with straps earlier if needed.

## Phase 4 — Weeks 10–14: return to the wall

**Entry criteria (criteria-based, not calendar-based):**
- Full bodyweight hang 3 × 20 s pain-free.
- Floor push-ups pain-free.
- Grip strength ≥85–90% of the other side (dynamometer or Voltra max isometric pull as proxy).
- No apprehension on loaded extension.

### Weeks 10–12: reintroduction

- 2 short sessions/week, vertical or slab, jugs, 3–4 grades below your normal warm-up grade.
- **Static movement only.** No dynos, no deadpoints, no cutting loose. Lock off and reach.
- Down-climb everything. Do not drop off the wall; the fall onto an outstretched arm into a mat is the exact mechanism that did this.
- Avoid early: gastons and wide compression (varus/valgus), mantles and palm-down press-outs (loaded extension), steep roofs (high peak elbow loads), laybacks on the injured side.
- Top-rope on autobelay or with a patient belayer beats bouldering for the first 2–3 weeks back purely because of fall exposure.

### Protect the fingers, not just the elbow

Your fingers were never injured, but a month of disuse deconditions the flexor tendons and A2/A4 pulleys, and connective tissue re-adapts to climbing-specific shear slower than muscle recovers. Isometric grip feeling fine in the cast does not mean the pulleys are ready for crimp loads. For the first few weeks back: open-hand/drag grip over crimp, big holds and good footholds, short sessions, no hangboard or board projecting, and avoid tweaky crimps and pockets for a couple of months. Treat any sharp, localised finger pain at the base of a digit as a stop signal in its own right. This dovetails with the elbow plan rather than competing with it, since both want low-grade, static, controlled early sessions.

### Weeks 12–14: rebuilding

- Add gentle overhang, smaller (but not crimpy-aggressive) holds, slightly more dynamic movement with two-handed catches first, one-arm catches last.
- Reintroduce falling deliberately: practise low, soft, tucked-arm landings before you risk an unexpected fall. Most climbing elbow dislocations are landing failures, not climbing failures.
- **Realistic expectation on full return:** week 14 is the optimistic end, not the target. The Physio Network climber case hit pre-injury grade at week 14 with close supervision, but the larger recreational-athlete data is more sobering: across 44 simple dislocations, mean time to return to pre-injury sport was ~22 weeks, with 82% returning. For a high-demand upper-limb sport like climbing, plan for a 4–5 month arc to *full* return and treat anything faster as a bonus. The bouldering cohort found 90% return to climbing but only two-thirds regain their previous grade, and fear of reinjury (not the joint) is the usual limiter.
- If you notice hesitation and overgripping, treat that as a training variable: rehearse the scary move types (committing reaches, controlled falls) at easy grades on purpose.

### Ongoing (week 14+)

- Keep 2×/week antagonist and elbow work (pressing, reverse wrist curls, pronation/supination) for at least 6 months; the ligament keeps remodelling for ~a year.
- Max-effort dynamic moves, one-arm catches, campusing: earliest ~week 16, and only with everything else clean.

## Monitoring

- Track morning elbow stiffness and pain (0–10) daily in Crimpd notes alongside Oura readiness; a pain score that trends up over 3+ days means hold or step back a phase.
- Weekly: measure extension deficit against the good arm (photo against a wall works), grip strength if you have a dynamometer.
- Book a physio review around week 6 and before first wall session; a varus/valgus stability check and posterolateral pivot apprehension test before Phase 4 is cheap insurance given stability was never formally graded.

## Tracker app: suggestions for the HTML tick-off page (Claude Code handoff)

These are suggestions, not a fixed spec. Adjust freely; clinic findings on 19 June (block schedule, stability grading) may change phase timing and gate criteria, so build those as editable config, not hardcoded values.

### Core concept

Single self-contained HTML file, localStorage persistence, openable from a bookmark. No backend. Mobile-friendly since most ticks happen at the gym or on the sofa.

### Suggested features

1. **"Today" view as the default screen.** Show only the current phase's exercises with sets/reps and checkboxes; everything else collapsed. One short list per day drives adherence; the full protocol lives behind a tab.
2. **Criteria-gated phases, not date-gated.** Each phase is a locked card listing its entry criteria as checkboxes (e.g. "extension within 10° of left", "3 × 20 s bodyweight hang pain-free"). Phase unlocks only when all are ticked. Dates display as guide ranges, never as the unlock mechanism. Make criteria editable since the physio may add or change gates.
3. **Daily metrics with trend lines.** Morning stiffness (0–10), pain (0–10), and a weekly extension-deficit entry (degrees, estimated from a photo against a wall). Plot with EMA smoothing rather than raw values; a rising EMA over ~5 days triggers a visible "hold, don't progress" banner. Optional manual fields for Oura readiness and bodyweight if cross-referencing is wanted.
4. **The pain rule enforced in UI.** A daily prompt: "did yesterday's session settle by this morning?" Answering no greys out progression buttons for the day and shows "repeat the same load." Makes the rule structural instead of aspirational.
5. **Brace block schedule widget.** Editable rows of date → allowed extension angle, with the current allowance shown prominently on the Today view. Updates whenever the clinic adjusts the block.
6. **Red-flag check, lightweight.** A weekly three-item prompt (ROM going backwards? clunking/giving way? ulnar tingling?). Any yes shows a persistent "contact fracture clinic" banner. Don't make it daily; it becomes noise.
7. **Milestone screens.** Treat a handful of moments as celebrations with their own screen: first full extension, first floor push-up, first bodyweight hang, first wall session, first session at previous grade. The wall-readiness checklist (Phase 4 entry criteria) deserves the biggest one.
8. **Streaks and a phase progress bar,** but tie the streak to "logged today" rather than "completed everything" so a deload day doesn't break it.
9. **Session timer conveniences:** a 10 s isometric repeat timer and a 3–5 min countdown for the long-duration extension holds.
10. **Data export/import as JSON** so history survives browser changes and can be fed back to Claude for analysis (e.g. EMA trend review before physio appointments).
11. **Content sourced from this file.** Parse or hand-copy the phase/exercise structure from this markdown so the protocol text stays the single source of truth; if the protocol changes after the clinic visit, regenerate the app content rather than editing two places.

### Things deliberately out of scope

- No localStorage alternatives needed (single user, single device is fine; export covers backup).
- No auth, no sync, no notifications. If a daily reminder is wanted, a recurring phone alarm beats building one.

## Sources behind the structure

- Schreiber et al. 2015, overhead motion protocol for simple dislocations (J Hand Surg): motion within 1 week, final arc 6–137°, no recurrent instability.
- Ross et al. 1999, immediate motion protocol (Naval Academy cohort): full ROM reached at ~19 days, all returned to unrestricted activity.
- FuncSie trial: early mobilisation vs plaster, faster return to sport, equal complications.
- Manocha et al. 2022, overhead positioning biomechanics with MCL/LCL injury (J Hand Ther): gravity compresses and stabilises the joint overhead.
- Nolte et al. 2023, recreational athletes after simple dislocation (J Orthop Surg Res): mean return to sport ~22 weeks, 82% returned.
- Müller et al. 2024, return to sport after bouldering elbow dislocations (Arch Orthop Trauma Surg): 90% return, two-thirds regain grade.
- HO literature (J Hand Ther therapist guide; Leyder et al. 2024): forced passive motion drives HO; active motion is protective.
- Climbing finger/pulley loading principles (The Climbing Doctor, Sheffield Climbing Clinic, climbing.com): graded open-hand loading, easy holds first, slow tendon adaptation.
- Physio Network climber case study and Val Jones's Sheffield regime/elbow masterclass.
- The Complete Climber, return-to-climbing movement progression post-dislocation.
